I'm an independent researcher in Antarctic meteorology and forecast verification, working outside an institution — by route and by choice.

The poles warm about twice as fast as the rest of the planet, and Antarctica holds the largest ice mass on Earth. Getting its weather right matters — for sea level, for iceberg formation, and for the AI forecast models now trusted where there's almost nothing to check them against.

Current study

What real station data reveals

Three-way wind-speed verification across Antarctic stations

  • The finding. I compared GraphCast (via WeatherBench2) with ERA5 reanalysis and SCAR READER station observations for near-surface wind across five stations. Verified against reanalysis, the forecast looks accurate; verified against the stations, real errors appear. Reanalysis-based verification masks them.
  • Why it matters. A caution against over-trusting AI models such as AIFS where observations are sparse — and a reminder that ground truth has the last word.
  • Status. Accepted for EMS2026 in Utrecht — the largest meteorology conference in Europe — and for NL Polar Day 2026. Packaged as a reusable notebook that re-runs at any low-altitude SCAR READER station.

See the working code & notebook: github.com/ElenaGuk/Portfolio.

Method

Research without a department

I set the direction

With no supervisor, I read the frontier — recent conference programmes and the current literature — to find what's genuinely live, then frame a question I can answer alone.

I build from open data

Station feeds, reanalysis, and model output, brought together in Python. Most of what I need is openly available; some of it takes an API.

I verify, twice

I check every step against published methods, because independent work earns its credibility on the results — not on a title or an affiliation.

I put it in the room

I present at conferences and release reusable notebooks, so the work can be re-run, checked, and built on by anyone.
