How it works

Put it all down in details, like a brief, with no "this is probably trivial." Nothing here is trivial. I'll ask follow-up questions. Next, the exact price is set by how complex the agreed task is.

This is where the deep analysis starts: I dig through local aggregators, resources in a country's language, maps, weather archives, and check the task against reality.

Why me, not AI

AI is useful. Just not for this.

I use AI in my own work. But an accessible trip turns on a few things an AI planner, by its nature, can't do.

I verify it on the ground.

AI works from what's published — listings, reviews, landing pages. And "accessible" in a listing often means "we'd like to think so." I check whether the step-free entrance is really step-free and the beach entry is really flat.

I own the outcome.

AI hands you a plausible plan with no stake in whether it works. I'm a real person who's accountable for all handed information being correct and up-to-date.

I plan for you, not the average tourist.

Accessibility is the opposite of average: the precise fit between one person's sensory thresholds, mobility, and conditions and what's actually there. And I'll tell you honestly when a destination won't work — then find one that will.

I understand it from the inside.

AI has general knowledge about sensory overload and the cost of it is unpredictable plan. I design around constraints because I personally live and travel with constraints.

Weather as an accessibility tool

Sometimes, weather is whether you can go outside at all. As a meteorologist, I know how to build a trip around that.

Heat intolerance, heart and breathing conditions, chronic fatigue, limited mobility — for any of these, the weather on the day decides whether a place is usable or off-limits. Standard travel advice treats weather as a comfort preference; I treat it as a planning constraint, and I work from the long-run climate pattern for your exact place and dates, not next week's forecast.

  • Heat measured the way the body feels it.I work from the felt temperature — humidity, sun and wind together, not the bare number — so for someone who can't cool down or move away quickly, heat stress is planned out of the itinerary from the start.
  • Windows you can rely on.I look for periods where the pattern holds steady from year to year, so there's less chance of a plan-breaking surprise — which matters most when the day has to be predictable.
  • The right hours, or the right place.Sometimes the fix is timing an outing to a morning window; sometimes it's a different destination altogether. I tell you which, and show you the data behind it.
  • Backed by research, not an app.I work as an independent meteorology researcher; my Antarctic forecast-verification study was accepted at EMS2026, Europe's largest meteorology conference. The same kind of multi-year climate data I use there is what I use to find your comfort windows. See the research →
