Independent research · Neurodiversity & work

Real competence, read through the wrong lens — twice.

I'm an independent researcher studying how autistic first-generation migrants are read at work — and why their skills so often don't reach the people doing the hiring.

When someone is both neurodivergent and a newcomer, two filters stack: a workplace's assumptions about how a "good" employee communicates, and a new country's assumptions about how work is done. Competence gets discounted through both at once. I call it the double-untranslated problem — and I research what changes it.

The research

The double-untranslated problem

What the two lenses cost — and what to do about it

  • The thesis. The skills are intact; the distortion is in the lens. Competence is misread through a combined cultural and neurotypical frame, so it never reaches the hiring decision at full strength.
  • Why it matters. Anti-discrimination law mostly protects people once they're hired — not from being quietly screened out first. Naming the mechanism is where changing it starts.
  • Status. A full research proposal written and submitted for independent research funding (ISRF); presented and connected at the Neurodiversity in Business Conference (London); now being developed into a PhD.

Method

Research without a department

I set the direction

With no supervisor, I read the frontier — recent conferences and the current literature — to find what's genuinely live, then frame a question I can pursue alone.

I go to the source

Lived accounts and the primary literature, not second-hand summaries. The people who've met the barrier know its shape best.

I verify, twice

I check every step against published methods, because independent work earns its credibility on the results — not on a title or an affiliation.

I put it in the room

I present at conferences and share materials others can use, so the work can be checked, applied, and built on.
